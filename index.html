Hands-on Tinkering and Tinkerability criteria

This post contains my findings on the tinkerability of the Makedo, littleBits, and Meccano tinkering materials. These materials are reviewed from the tinkerability viewpoints of preciousness, interoperability, self-guiding, level of instruction (skills, tools) needed, collaboratability and sensory engagement.

Criteria Makedo littleBits Meccano
Preciousness ⭐The main material used, cardboard, is not precious at all and can be cut, ripped, modified, destroyed in any way imaginable without the kit losing any inherent value. The screws are single-piece parts that will possibly lose their purpose of connecting cardboard if modified. ⭐⭐⭐Sophisticated circuit pieces will lose all of their value if they are modified in a way that breaks their electrical functionality. At that point, they serve no purpose in the kit. ⭐⭐These metal pieces and screws hold little inherent preciousness and could be cut and bent in different ways so that they can still be connected together. Some special parts such as wheels and screws could lose preciousness if altered in a destructive way.
Interoperability ⭐⭐⭐Very interoperable as the main part of the kit is mostly plastic screws. There is no inherent requirement to use cardboard with the screws. ⭐⭐Could be interoperable with other electronics if the wires in the building blocks were to be exposed. ⭐⭐⭐Very interoperable with other kinds of tinkering materials. Especially if used together with screws in which case the materials could be attached to other kinds of materials.
Self-Guiding ⭐⭐⭐This set does not require any outside guidance. The user is free to provide the main cardboard materials themselves and cut them into shape, connecting them with screws in any way possible. ⭐⭐With common knowledge on circuits, this kit can be used without much guidance. However, the kit also feels the need to provide some example circuits to build. ⭐⭐⭐The maker kit allows for a lot of self-guidance since it provides no instructions and the pieces can be connected in almost any way imaginable. It is up to the user to decide how they build.
Level of Instruction (Skills, Tools) Needed ⭐⭐⭐The kit requires only a basic cardboard knife, a screwdriver, and the basic skill to use them. It is thus suitable for kids, too. ⭐⭐This kit requires a bit more understanding of how circuits work, although this is made easier by not exposing the wires and instead using magnets for connecting the circuit pieces. Tools are not required. ⭐⭐⭐This kit requires a basic screwdriver and the skill of using that to connect pieces, nothing else.
Collaboratability ⭐⭐⭐This kit can easily be used simultaneously with multiple people since the scale of building is quite large. It is probably thus easier to work on different parts of the build by separate people, combining the build at the end. ⭐More difficult for multiple people to work with simultaneously due to the small size of the kit elements. People will often get in each other’s way if they try to tinker at the same time. ⭐⭐Again, these pieces are quite small, so multiple people working on the same project might prove more difficult. These are easier to work with in parallel compared to littleBits.
Sensory Engagement ⭐This kit only really activates visual senses and not even those as much since, unless painted, the cardboard might not be visually too rich. ⭐⭐⭐This kit engages the most senses with its various sensors and outputs, including speakers, microphones, light sensors, and light outputs. These can be connected to activate simultaneously. ⭐⭐This kit engages mostly visual and perhaps haptic senses if more intricate mechanisms and special pieces are used.

Initial Reflection on Tinkering

This post contains some of my initial reflectioning and thoughts about tinkering before the course has properly kicked off.

1. Defining Tinkering, Trial-and-Error, and Brainstorming

Tinkering: "To work in the manner of a tinker. Especially: to repair, adjust, or work with something in an unskilled or experimental manner." (Merriam-Webster) | "Engaging in hands-on experimentation with an object, system, or idea, often without a fixed goal, to explore possibilities, make adjustments, or improve functionality." (Chat-GPT)

Trial-and-Error: "Finding out the best way to reach a desired result or a correct solution by trying out one or more ways or means and by noting and eliminating errors or causes of failure." (Merriam-Webster) | "A method of problem-solving that involves testing different approaches, learning from failures, and refining the solution through iteration." (Chat-GPT)

Brainstorming: "A group problem-solving technique that involves the spontaneous contribution of ideas from all members of the group." (Merriam-Webster) | "A creative process where individuals or groups generate and refine ideas through free-flowing, spontaneous discussion without immediate judgment." (Chat-GPT)


Definition of a 'tinkering mindset' by the course materials:
  • There is a required open-endedness (considering a solution space or result).
  • There is a notion of iteration (trial and error, incremental improvements and changes).
  • The process and mindset are active, hands-on, and experimental.

Comparison: The Merriam-Webster definitions emphasize the traditional and structured meanings of these concepts, while Chat-GPT's definitions focus on their practical and exploratory aspects. The course's "tinkering mindset" aligns closely with both but emphasizes open-ended exploration, iteration, and hands-on experimentation. Tinkering overlaps with trial-and-error but is more creative, and brainstorming shares elements of spontaneity but lacks the hands-on aspect of tinkering.

2. Technical Hurdles in an I-Tech Project

A typical I-Tech Related Project Case: Smart Clothing

Smart clothing combines fashion and technology, often integrating sensors and electronics into fabric to monitor health, track movement, or improve comfort.

  • 1. Power Supply and Battery Life:
    One of the biggest challenges in smart clothing is integrating a power supply without sacrificing comfort or flexibility. Finding lightweight, durable batteries that last long enough without needing frequent recharging is tricky. A playful approach could involve experimenting with alternative energy sources like kinetic or solar energy.
  • 2. Sensor Integration and Accuracy:
    Sensors need to be embedded in the fabric without compromising its wearability. Ensuring these sensors accurately collect data (like temperature, heart rate, or movement) is difficult. A playful approach could involve testing different sensor placements or materials to see how they interact with the clothing and influence data accuracy.
  • 3. Durability and Washing:
    Smart clothing must be durable enough to withstand regular washing and everyday wear. Making electronics waterproof and machine-washable is a challenge. This is an area where a playful approach could involve trial-and-error testing with different materials or protective coatings to find the most reliable solution.
  • 4. Comfort and Wearability:
    Adding technology to clothing may make it less comfortable, especially when it comes to sensors and wires. Designers need to balance functionality with comfort. A playful approach could be prototyping various designs, focusing on experimenting with different shapes, placements, and materials to optimize comfort while maintaining functionality.
  • 5. Data Processing and User Interface:
    The smart clothing will generate large amounts of data, and a key challenge is processing it and presenting it in an understandable way through an app or interface. A playful approach here could include iterative design of the interface, testing various data visualizations, and allowing room for user feedback to refine the product.

4. Some domains and limitations of applying tinkering

1. User Interface Design:
Tinkering in UI design could allow for exploration of different layouts, color schemes, and interaction models to improve user engagement and accessibility. This could be combined with computational user interface development to assess different UI designs with algorithms. However, limitations include the potential risk of creating overly complex, cluttered or confusing interfaces if not iterated properly.

2. Web User Interface Development:
Web UI development is a field where tinkering allows constant testing and refinement of responsive, dynamic elements, especially combined with automated CI/CD pipelines. An untinkerable domain here could involve ensuring security standards, which requires more structured and careful development than experimentation.

3. Requirements Defining in Software Projects:
Through iterative feedback and exploring different methods of requirement elicitation (e.g., user stories, prototyping, workshops), new ways to engage stakeholders and prioritize features can be discovered. The limitation is that tinkering may lead to scope creep or confusion, especially when stakeholder goals are misaligned.

4. Smart Watches:
Ideas like experimenting with novel haptic feedback systems, integrating environmental sensors, or exploring alternative input methods (e.g., skin gestures) can be tinkered with. However, limitations include battery life constraints and small screen sizes, which restrict how much data or functionality can be displayed to tinker with. The device dimensions, materials and other physical properties might not be as open to freeminded tinkering as its digital user interface.

5. Virtual Reality User Interface Development:
Tinkering with VR user interfaces could lead to more immersive experiences, using spatial gestures, eye-tracking, or neural interfaces that can be implemented as lo-fi prototypes first, tested and then possibly included if there is potential interacitonal value in them. Limitations come with the complexity of creating fluid, intuitive navigation and avoiding motion sickness.
