Inspecting Tinkering Critically

In this post I will do some critical reflecting on the limitations and possible side effects of tinkering through a series of reflective questions.

Tinkering is a way to generate knowledge. What kind of knowledge results from a tinkering process in your experience?

The knowledge generated through tinkering is often mostly experiences, feelings and observations about how applying specific changes to a system affects it. This kind of knowledge has a very low level of abstraction and is bound to the tinkering environment. The learned knowledge will not automatically translate into any bigger picture realization or universal experience, though tinkering can act as a launchpad for wider reflecting.

Tinkering with classical materials like clay, paper, colour, textiles is more related to craft. How does tinkering work with digital and electronic media?

The nature of tinkering itself is not limited to hands-on, physical materials. Iteratively experimenting and adjusting digital properties and characteristics of e.g. video, music or software can be considered tinkering just as well. However, it can be argued that tinkering with hands-on materials is more interactive and thus more thought-evoking and effective.

One basic characteristic of science is reproducibility. Is that possible with tinkering? Or do we want that at all?

Tinkering supports reproducibility to some extent. When given materials, tools, scaffolding and an objective, we can affect the tinkering process, experience and results of different individuals. However, one of the main goals of tinkering is a certain unknowness of these things. If we aim for complete reproducibility we lose that. But that is not to say that tinkering processes would be simply random, non-reproducible experiences.

Design practice changed during the past years, from long design phases to short, iterative processes, due to the availability of new prototyping technologies. Is this iterative process of prototyping qualitatively different from the cycles we do in tinkering?

Iterative design shares similarities with tinkering, since both of them focus on short term innovation, allow and encourage making mistakes and design-wise suboptimal decisions and focus on gradually improving/creating a product. The key difference between iterative design and tinkering is that tinkering does not require a specific goal in mind, whereas successful iterative design requires clearly defined objectives and quality metrics so that the designers know when is the right time to stop iterating.

Can a co-design session be described as a tinkering session as we treat it in our course?

Co-operative design refers to a design process which involves not only designers but also other stakeholders such as users and project managers. While tinkering aims to involve all participants of the session in the creation process in a similar way, the process is still different from a traditional design process. However, co-design sessions are one step closer to tinkering sessions since they emphasize involving everyday people to interact and participate in the design process.

The choice of materials (seed, tools, scaffolding, facilitation) has an effect on the outcome, the knowledge and products made in a tinkering session. How can we use that in a co-design session? Can we really influence the kind of results here, and do we want that?

Similar to setting up a tinkering playground setting with scaffolding materials and instructions, we can create a co-design setting that gives the participants ideas while steering them towards viable and actionable design solutions. Without the proper limitations and guidance both tinkering and co-design sessions turn into chaos which ultimately produces nothing of value. That is why we do want to pose some limitations materialwise to ensure meaningful sessions and end results.

In “research through design” can tinkering fill in the design part?

Research through design refers to conducting scientific research through doing hands-on design work and learning from it. Research through design is especially effective for researching two main things: design itself, and the behavior of people. This is because any research method that does not involve hands-on design work does not allow us to observe and learn from the design process and the people involved themselves. In that sense “research through tinkering” would be about researching 1) the tinkering process and 2) the behavior of people. Thus while tinkering and design processes are different, through both one can still research human behavior in those types of situations.

Are there design questions where tinkering is not a possible or useful approach?

Tinkering requires a certain level of hands-on exploration for it to be effective. When we are designing very high-level abstract things such as user experience or organization culture tinkering can even become impossible.

Read Invent to Learn (2013) page 41. How to balance real-world (criticality) with the fruitful mindset of tinkering?

(I have the 2019 edition of Invent To Learn so I do not know what section of the book that refers to). In a general sense real-world criticality and its limitations actually support creative processes. This is because uncontrolled and -limited creation and tinkering only results in chaos and/or meaningless results since the end products are not physically realistic, scalable or actionable in any other sense. Thus tinkering facilitators should not be afraid of setting limitations and offering controlled guidance. However, the important thing is that they must be aware of and recognize when they pose limitations or offer guidance since that can also happen unintentionally and in a harmful way to the creative process.

What is the critical impact your tinkering exercise could have? (on you, your design/engineering practice or problem, both positive and negative)

Our group project tinkering session is about building vibrating race robots. The scaffolding problem we are tacking is that we want to visualize and teach vibrational movement without the participants requiring to know anything about the electronics powering the vibration. A positive critical impact the session could have is that the participants also become interested in how the electronics work in the process. A possible negative impact is that they lose interest in the vibrational movement altogether, if the electronics or the scaffolding material we provide them are still too complex to use and work with.

What is the impact your session has regarding (more) stuff, ecological footprint and impact on our planet (i.e. how can you avoid that STEM workshops with waste material result in more waste material?)

Our vibrating race robots session is not tied to any other materials apart from the electronics of the seed we provide them. All other materials and tools can be chosen in an environmentally friendly way and recycled afterwards. The most environmentally negative impact our session has is that it is battery powered. We use a 9 volt battery to power two DC motors. This means that the battery runs out in approximately two sessions and it then has to be replaced. For this we should have a battery recharger in hand and make sure that the participants do not leave the motors on by accident or too long.

Reflecting on Tinkering and Facilitation

This section explores facilitation, learning , and my personal tinkering in design and engineering processes.

Facilitation Approaches for Adults

The LEGO Serious Play guide defines facilitation as “making something (difficult) easier.” In LEGO Serious Play, the role of a facilitator is to drive the facilitated process by helping people explore, learn, and develop. The two most important things to keep in mind as a facilitator are:

  • Focus on open-ended building challenges and allowing room for questions, reflections, and dialogue.
  • Utilizing the physical LEGO bricks fully as facilitation tools, encouraging hands-on engagement in any way imaginable.

When it comes to adults, it might be more difficult to get them to engage with LEGOs or other toy-like facilitation tools. They might find the concept humorous and struggle to fully engage in the process. The perceived silliness, paired with a fear of making mistakes with children’s toys, could make people more passive and restrict innovation and discussion.

Key Principles of Papert and Montessori

Papert’s Constructionist Learning Principles

  1. Learn by doing instead of observing.
  2. Building with technology can elevate learning.
  3. Learning should be fun but not too easy.
  4. Take charge of your personal learning.
  5. Manage your personal time yourself.
  6. You must feel free to make mistakes in order to succeed.
  7. Involve yourself in the teaching.
  8. Learn enough about technology to fully utilize it when learning.

Montessori’s Learning Principles

Italian doctor of pedagogy Maria Montessori also developed ten principles based on respect for children’s learning process:

  1. Respect children; their uniqueness and freedom.
  2. Children absorb information best during their first 6 years.
  3. Children show sensitive periods when they learn better than at other times.
  4. Children should learn wholly—intellectually, physically, emotionally, and socially.
  5. Learning should be individualized.
  6. Children should not be constrained and should be free to move and interact.
  7. Places of learning should be prepared and thoughtfully designed.
  8. Learning should be intrinsically motivating.
  9. Independence should guide children's learning.
  10. Trust in children learning by themselves when provided stimuli.

Designing Effective Tinkering Spaces

Chapter 11 of ‘Invent to learn’ describes shaping learning and tinkering environments successfully:

  • Designing a space that is both functional and flexible.
  • Prioritizing security and safety.
  • Ensuring enough project storage.
  • Setting rules and protocols on how parts and tools are shared or distributed.
  • Providing flexible accessibility instead of rigid operating hours.

A design lab does not necessarily need the latest tools or abundant materials. Instead, it should be an accessible, safe, and inspiring space with some stimuli for creation.

My Tinkering Practices as an Engineer

I most naturally do tinkering in the middle phases of development or design. This is when I have already spent some time on the first iteration. At that point, I am confident enough with the state of the product to assess whether there is potential in it or not. Tinkering with too many ideas too early is difficult for me because I feel like many of my better improvement ideas stem after I have focused on a limited scope for some time.

One natural way for me to get unstuck in engineering/design problems is to get out of my head by either drawing notes of what I am currently thinking (thought-dumping) or by taking a piece of paper and trying to draw the ideas I am thinking of. I feel like this gives me more space to think and helps me get back to my flow process as I do not have to juggle the previous ideas in my head anymore.

Initial Reflection on Tinkering

This post contains some of my initial reflectioning and thoughts about tinkering before the course has properly kicked off.

1. Defining Tinkering, Trial-and-Error, and Brainstorming

Tinkering: "To work in the manner of a tinker. Especially: to repair, adjust, or work with something in an unskilled or experimental manner." (Merriam-Webster) | "Engaging in hands-on experimentation with an object, system, or idea, often without a fixed goal, to explore possibilities, make adjustments, or improve functionality." (Chat-GPT)

Trial-and-Error: "Finding out the best way to reach a desired result or a correct solution by trying out one or more ways or means and by noting and eliminating errors or causes of failure." (Merriam-Webster) | "A method of problem-solving that involves testing different approaches, learning from failures, and refining the solution through iteration." (Chat-GPT)

Brainstorming: "A group problem-solving technique that involves the spontaneous contribution of ideas from all members of the group." (Merriam-Webster) | "A creative process where individuals or groups generate and refine ideas through free-flowing, spontaneous discussion without immediate judgment." (Chat-GPT)


Definition of a 'tinkering mindset' by the course materials:
  • There is a required open-endedness (considering a solution space or result).
  • There is a notion of iteration (trial and error, incremental improvements and changes).
  • The process and mindset are active, hands-on, and experimental.

Comparison: The Merriam-Webster definitions emphasize the traditional and structured meanings of these concepts, while Chat-GPT's definitions focus on their practical and exploratory aspects. The course's "tinkering mindset" aligns closely with both but emphasizes open-ended exploration, iteration, and hands-on experimentation. Tinkering overlaps with trial-and-error but is more creative, and brainstorming shares elements of spontaneity but lacks the hands-on aspect of tinkering.

2. Technical Hurdles in an I-Tech Project

A typical I-Tech Related Project Case: Smart Clothing

Smart clothing combines fashion and technology, often integrating sensors and electronics into fabric to monitor health, track movement, or improve comfort.

  • 1. Power Supply and Battery Life:
    One of the biggest challenges in smart clothing is integrating a power supply without sacrificing comfort or flexibility. Finding lightweight, durable batteries that last long enough without needing frequent recharging is tricky. A playful approach could involve experimenting with alternative energy sources like kinetic or solar energy.
  • 2. Sensor Integration and Accuracy:
    Sensors need to be embedded in the fabric without compromising its wearability. Ensuring these sensors accurately collect data (like temperature, heart rate, or movement) is difficult. A playful approach could involve testing different sensor placements or materials to see how they interact with the clothing and influence data accuracy.
  • 3. Durability and Washing:
    Smart clothing must be durable enough to withstand regular washing and everyday wear. Making electronics waterproof and machine-washable is a challenge. This is an area where a playful approach could involve trial-and-error testing with different materials or protective coatings to find the most reliable solution.
  • 4. Comfort and Wearability:
    Adding technology to clothing may make it less comfortable, especially when it comes to sensors and wires. Designers need to balance functionality with comfort. A playful approach could be prototyping various designs, focusing on experimenting with different shapes, placements, and materials to optimize comfort while maintaining functionality.
  • 5. Data Processing and User Interface:
    The smart clothing will generate large amounts of data, and a key challenge is processing it and presenting it in an understandable way through an app or interface. A playful approach here could include iterative design of the interface, testing various data visualizations, and allowing room for user feedback to refine the product.

4. Some domains and limitations of applying tinkering

1. User Interface Design:
Tinkering in UI design could allow for exploration of different layouts, color schemes, and interaction models to improve user engagement and accessibility. This could be combined with computational user interface development to assess different UI designs with algorithms. However, limitations include the potential risk of creating overly complex, cluttered or confusing interfaces if not iterated properly.

2. Web User Interface Development:
Web UI development is a field where tinkering allows constant testing and refinement of responsive, dynamic elements, especially combined with automated CI/CD pipelines. An untinkerable domain here could involve ensuring security standards, which requires more structured and careful development than experimentation.

3. Requirements Defining in Software Projects:
Through iterative feedback and exploring different methods of requirement elicitation (e.g., user stories, prototyping, workshops), new ways to engage stakeholders and prioritize features can be discovered. The limitation is that tinkering may lead to scope creep or confusion, especially when stakeholder goals are misaligned.

4. Smart Watches:
Ideas like experimenting with novel haptic feedback systems, integrating environmental sensors, or exploring alternative input methods (e.g., skin gestures) can be tinkered with. However, limitations include battery life constraints and small screen sizes, which restrict how much data or functionality can be displayed to tinker with. The device dimensions, materials and other physical properties might not be as open to freeminded tinkering as its digital user interface.

5. Virtual Reality User Interface Development:
Tinkering with VR user interfaces could lead to more immersive experiences, using spatial gestures, eye-tracking, or neural interfaces that can be implemented as lo-fi prototypes first, tested and then possibly included if there is potential interacitonal value in them. Limitations come with the complexity of creating fluid, intuitive navigation and avoiding motion sickness.
